Natural & Whole Numbers

Learn about natural and whole numbers! 📚

Natural numbers and whole numbers are the foundation of mathematics. Understanding these basic concepts helps us work with more advanced topics. Natural numbers are the counting numbers (1, 2, 3, ...), while whole numbers include zero along with natural numbers (0, 1, 2, 3, ...). In this chapter, you'll explore the properties and operations with these numbers.

📖 What You'll Learn:

  • Understanding natural numbers and whole numbers
  • Properties of addition and multiplication
  • Representing numbers on a number line
  • Commutative and associative properties
  • Distributive property of multiplication
  • Operations with whole numbers and their closure properties
  • Pattern recognition and matching problems

🔒 Closure Property

Definition: A set is closed under an operation if performing that operation on any two elements of the set always gives a result that is also in the set.

Addition:

  • Whole Numbers: 3 + 5 = 8 ✓ (result is a whole number)
  • Whole Numbers: 0 + 7 = 7 ✓ (result is a whole number)
  • Closure: YES - Adding any two whole numbers always gives a whole number

Subtraction:

  • Whole Numbers: 5 - 3 = 2 ✓ (result is a whole number)
  • Whole Numbers: 3 - 5 = -2 ✗ (NOT a whole number!)
  • Closure: NO - Subtracting whole numbers doesn't always give a whole number

Multiplication:

  • Whole Numbers: 4 × 6 = 24 ✓ (result is a whole number)
  • Whole Numbers: 0 × 9 = 0 ✓ (result is a whole number)
  • Closure: YES - Multiplying any two whole numbers always gives a whole number

Division:

  • Whole Numbers: 8 ÷ 2 = 4 ✓ (result is a whole number)
  • Whole Numbers: 7 ÷ 2 = 3.5 ✗ (NOT a whole number!)
  • Closure: NO - Dividing whole numbers doesn't always give a whole number

🔄 Commutative Property

Definition: The order of elements doesn't change the result. If a ⊕ b = b ⊕ a, then the operation is commutative.

Addition:

  • 5 + 3 = 8 and 3 + 5 = 8 ✓
  • 12 + 7 = 19 and 7 + 12 = 19 ✓
  • Commutative: YES - a + b = b + a (Order doesn't matter)

Subtraction:

  • 10 - 4 = 6 but 4 - 10 = -6 ✗
  • 8 - 5 = 3 but 5 - 8 = -3 ✗
  • Commutative: NO - a - b ≠ b - a (Order DOES matter)

Multiplication:

  • 6 × 4 = 24 and 4 × 6 = 24 ✓
  • 3 × 8 = 24 and 8 × 3 = 24 ✓
  • Commutative: YES - a × b = b × a (Order doesn't matter)

Division:

  • 20 ÷ 4 = 5 but 4 ÷ 20 = 0.2 ✗
  • 12 ÷ 3 = 4 but 3 ÷ 12 = 0.25 ✗
  • Commutative: NO - a ÷ b ≠ b ÷ a (Order DOES matter)

🔗 Associative Property

Definition: The grouping of elements doesn't change the result. If (a ⊕ b) ⊕ c = a ⊕ (b ⊕ c), then the operation is associative.

Addition:

  • (2 + 3) + 4 = 5 + 4 = 9 and 2 + (3 + 4) = 2 + 7 = 9 ✓
  • (5 + 6) + 2 = 11 + 2 = 13 and 5 + (6 + 2) = 5 + 8 = 13 ✓
  • Associative: YES - (a + b) + c = a + (b + c) (Grouping doesn't matter)

Subtraction:

  • (10 - 5) - 2 = 5 - 2 = 3 but 10 - (5 - 2) = 10 - 3 = 7 ✗
  • (20 - 8) - 3 = 12 - 3 = 9 but 20 - (8 - 3) = 20 - 5 = 15 ✗
  • Associative: NO - (a - b) - c ≠ a - (b - c) (Grouping DOES matter)

Multiplication:

  • (2 × 3) × 4 = 6 × 4 = 24 and 2 × (3 × 4) = 2 × 12 = 24 ✓
  • (5 × 2) × 3 = 10 × 3 = 30 and 5 × (2 × 3) = 5 × 6 = 30 ✓
  • Associative: YES - (a × b) × c = a × (b × c) (Grouping doesn't matter)

Division:

  • (16 ÷ 4) ÷ 2 = 4 ÷ 2 = 2 but 16 ÷ (4 ÷ 2) = 16 ÷ 2 = 8 ✗
  • (20 ÷ 5) ÷ 2 = 4 ÷ 2 = 2 but 20 ÷ (5 ÷ 2) = 20 ÷ 2.5 = 8 ✗
  • Associative: NO - (a ÷ b) ÷ c ≠ a ÷ (b ÷ c) (Grouping DOES matter)

📦 Distributive Property

Definition: Multiplication distributes over addition/subtraction. a × (b + c) = (a × b) + (a × c) and a × (b - c) = (a × b) - (a × c)

Multiplication Over Addition:

  • Example 1: 5 × (3 + 2) = 5 × 5 = 25
  • Distributed: (5 × 3) + (5 × 2) = 15 + 10 = 25 ✓
  • Example 2: 4 × (6 + 1) = 4 × 7 = 28
  • Distributed: (4 × 6) + (4 × 1) = 24 + 4 = 28 ✓

Multiplication Over Subtraction:

  • Example 1: 7 × (8 - 3) = 7 × 5 = 35
  • Distributed: (7 × 8) - (7 × 3) = 56 - 21 = 35 ✓
  • Example 2: 6 × (10 - 4) = 6 × 6 = 36
  • Distributed: (6 × 10) - (6 × 4) = 60 - 24 = 36 ✓

Real-World Application:

  • Problem: If you buy 5 boxes with (3 apples + 2 oranges) each, how many fruits total?
  • Method 1: 5 × (3 + 2) = 5 × 5 = 25 fruits
  • Method 2: (5 × 3) + (5 × 2) = 15 + 10 = 25 fruits ✓
  • Distributive: YES - Both methods give the same answer!

➕ Additive Identity

Definition: The additive identity is the number that, when added to any number, gives that same number. The additive identity is 0.

Formula: a + 0 = a and 0 + a = a

  • 5 + 0 = 5 ✓ (The number stays the same)
  • 0 + 12 = 12 ✓ (The number stays the same)
  • 100 + 0 = 100 ✓ (Adding zero doesn't change anything)
  • 0 + 0 = 0 ✓ (Zero added to zero is still zero)

💡 Why is it important?

  • Zero is called the "additive identity" because it's the "identity" for addition
  • It preserves the identity (value) of any number you add it to
  • This property works for all integers, fractions, and real numbers

✖️ Multiplicative Identity

Definition: The multiplicative identity is the number that, when multiplied by any number, gives that same number. The multiplicative identity is 1.

Formula: a × 1 = a and 1 × a = a

  • 7 × 1 = 7 ✓ (The number stays the same)
  • 1 × 25 = 25 ✓ (The number stays the same)
  • 100 × 1 = 100 ✓ (Multiplying by 1 doesn't change anything)
  • 1 × 1 = 1 ✓ (One multiplied by one is still one)

💡 Why is it important?

  • One is called the "multiplicative identity" because it's the "identity" for multiplication
  • It preserves the identity (value) of any number you multiply it by
  • This property works for all integers, fractions, and real numbers

🔄➖ Additive Inverse

Definition: The additive inverse of a number is another number that, when added to the original, gives the additive identity (0). For a number a, its additive inverse is -a.

Formula: a + (-a) = 0 and (-a) + a = 0

  • Example 1: The additive inverse of 5 is -5, because 5 + (-5) = 0 ✓
  • Example 2: The additive inverse of -8 is 8, because -8 + 8 = 0 ✓
  • Example 3: The additive inverse of 12 is -12, because 12 + (-12) = 0 ✓
  • Example 4: The additive inverse of 0 is 0, because 0 + 0 = 0 ✓

💡 Real-World Application:

  • Banking: If you deposit $100 (earn +100), and then withdraw $100 (spend -100), you end up with 0 net change
  • Temperature: If it's 10°C above zero and drops 10°C below zero, it's back to 0°C
  • Games: If you gain 5 points and lose 5 points, you're back to your starting score

🔄✖️ Multiplicative Inverse

Definition: The multiplicative inverse of a number is another number that, when multiplied by the original, gives the multiplicative identity (1). For a number a, its multiplicative inverse is 1/a (also written as a⁻¹).

Formula: a × (1/a) = 1 and (1/a) × a = 1 (where a ≠ 0)

  • Example 1: The multiplicative inverse of 5 is 1/5 (or 0.2), because 5 × (1/5) = 1 ✓
  • Example 2: The multiplicative inverse of 2 is 1/2 (or 0.5), because 2 × (1/2) = 1 ✓
  • Example 3: The multiplicative inverse of 10 is 1/10 (or 0.1), because 10 × (1/10) = 1 ✓
  • Example 4: The multiplicative inverse of 1/4 is 4, because (1/4) × 4 = 1 ✓

⚠️ Special Case - Zero:

  • Zero has NO multiplicative inverse
  • There is no number that when multiplied by 0 gives 1
  • 0 × (any number) = 0, never 1

💡 Real-World Application:

  • Recipes: If a recipe serves 5 people and you multiply it by (1/5), you get a recipe for 1 person
  • Photography: If an image is enlarged by 4×, reducing it by 1/4 returns it to original size
  • Speed: If you're traveling at 60 km/h and multiply by 1/60, you get the distance covered per minute

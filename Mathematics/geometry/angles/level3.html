Level 3: Measuring Angles

Learn to measure angles with precision! 📐

📊 What is a Degree?

A degree is the unit of measurement we use for angles. The symbol is °.

A degree is 1/360th of a full circle. That means:

🔵 One Full Circle

360°

A complete rotation

⬜ Right Angle

90°

A quarter circle

↔️ Straight Angle

180°

A half circle

📐 Three-Quarter Turn

270°

Three-quarters circle

🎯 Understanding the Circle as 360°

Imagine a clock or a compass. If you start facing North and turn all the way around until you face North again, you've made a complete turn of 360°.

  • North to East: 90° (quarter turn)
  • North to South: 180° (half turn)
  • North to West: 270° (three-quarter turn)
  • North to North: 360° (full turn)

📏 The Protractor: Your Angle-Measuring Tool

A protractor is a special tool used to measure and draw angles. It's a semicircle (half circle) with degree markings from 0° to 180°.

🔍 Parts of a Protractor

  • Semicircular shape: Curved edge with degree markings
  • Degree markings: Numbers from 0° to 180° (and sometimes both directions)
  • Center point: A small dot or hole where you place the vertex
  • Straight edge: The flat bottom side (0° line)
  • Two scales: Outer scale (0-180 going one direction) and inner scale (0-180 going the other direction)

📋 Steps to Measure an Angle with a Protractor

  1. Place the vertex: Put the center point of the protractor exactly on the vertex (corner) of the angle
  2. Align the baseline: Line up one arm of the angle with the 0° line (the straight edge)
  3. Read the scale: See where the other arm of the angle crosses the degree markings
  4. Check your answer: Make sure you're reading the correct scale (inner or outer)
  5. Record the measurement: Write down the number with the degree symbol (°)
💡 Pro Tip: Most protractors have two scales. Always check which one your angle aligns with! If your angle opens to the right, use one scale; if it opens to the left, use the other.

🎯 Estimating Angles (Without a Protractor)

Sometimes you don't have a protractor, so you can estimate angles by comparing them to familiar angles:

Reference Angles to Remember

  • 90° (Right Angle): The corner of a square, book, or window. Use this as your main reference!
  • 180° (Straight Angle): A straight line, like the horizon
  • 45°: Halfway between 0° and 90° - the diagonal of a square
  • 60°: The angle in an equilateral triangle
  • 30°: Smaller than 45°, like one hour on a clock

How to Estimate

  • If the angle looks like a 90° corner, it's close to 90°
  • If it's smaller than 90°, estimate how much smaller (maybe 45°, 60°, or 30°?)
  • If it's larger than 90°, compare to 180° to estimate
  • Practice makes perfect! The more you estimate, the better you become

✏️ Drawing Angles with a Protractor

You can also use a protractor to draw angles of a specific size:

📋 Steps to Draw an Angle

  1. Draw a starting line: Draw a horizontal line (this will be one arm)
  2. Mark the vertex: Choose a point on this line to be the vertex
  3. Place the protractor: Put the center of the protractor on the vertex
  4. Find your angle: Mark a dot where the desired angle measurement is (e.g., 60°)
  5. Draw the second arm: Use a ruler to connect the vertex to the dot you marked
  6. Label it: Write the angle measurement on your drawing

Example Angles to Draw

  • 30° - A sharp angle (small opening)
  • 60° - A moderate angle
  • 90° - A perfect right angle
  • 120° - A wide angle
  • 150° - An even wider angle

🌡️ Temperature vs Angles (Fun Analogy!)

You might see the degree symbol (°) used for temperature too. But in angles, we're measuring the opening of an angle, not how hot something is!

  • Temperature degree: 25°C (degrees Celsius)
  • Angle degree: 45° (angle measurement)

💡 Remember: Always pay attention to the context to know if you're talking about temperature or angles!
